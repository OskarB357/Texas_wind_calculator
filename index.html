Wind Energy Impact Calculator

People often state that renewable energy is the future and claim it is a straightforward solution to environmental problems. The solutions are painted as clean, harmless, and beneficial to all. However, this framing, which may be helpful for implementing renewable energy, leaves out many of the social and environmental problems associated with this new form of energy. Some scholars attempt to change this common belief that renewable energy automatically leads to improvement. Writers like Howe, Lennon, and Hardin directly and indirectly display that different forms of energy bring many different tradeoffs and consequences. The goal of the wind-energy calculator is to visualize the benefits and drawbacks of this specific renewable energy, bringing up points from the scholars, so that users can form their own conclusions about wind energy.

      Cymene Howe's Ecologics

      Cymene Howe's Ecologics illustrates how wind energy projects can affect lands and people in multiple ways. Her work is based in southern Mexico, a region that is very popular for wind energy due to stronger winds. She explains that the meaning of wind has changed as the number of turbines has increased. It was once understood as a natural force, but now it is seen as something that can be extracted or a resource. This change brings new expectations of what the purpose of wind is, who can use it, and who can benefit from it. Howe describes land owners like Don Jose, who has leased parts of his property to a wind company. This allows him to have a steady income and live better, claiming that he believes his prosperity comes from the income generated from the land. However, the surrounding communities do not share these same advantages. They have to live with the noise, dust, construction, and changing landscape due to the wind turbines, and do not receive any compensation. Other communities face pressure from companies looking for access to property to place wind turbines. Howe's writing shows that wind energy cannot just be understood through pounds of carbon saved or megawatt output, but also as a force that affects communities, and benefits some while harming others. This complexity is shown in the tool through its consideration of environmental impacts, the amount of land needed, and sound level, on top of just energy produced, which provides a broader evaluation of whether wind turbines are truly beneficial. This helps people understand what the communities and people who live near the turbines, like in Howe's writing, are affected by the turbines rather than just what large corporations see.

      Lennon's "Subjects of the Sun"

      Lennon's "Subjects of the Sun: Urban Solarities and the Governance of Life in the Shadows of Racial Capitalism" shows how renewable energy can reinforce preexisting inequalities. He focuses on the solar-powered microgrid in the Marcus Garvey Apartments in Brownsville, New York. He writes about how the apartments have promotional images presenting the solar panels as clean and sustainable, but the residents themselves were not even aware of the microgrid. This reveals a gap between the presentation for the project and the people who actually benefit from it. One interesting component that Lennon sheds light on is that people often encounter renewable energy only through digital images and not through physical interaction. He claims that this system of renewable energy is often understood through a visual economy, which impresses people through its aesthetics rather than through physical experience or technical understanding. Lennon also claims that solar manufacturing depends on supply chains that involve toxic chemicals. Connecting this to wind turbines, wind turbines are often depicted in beautiful green fields in an attempt to display their connection to natural settings and environmental benefit. Almost never are there images of wind turbines being produced in factories or assembled through industrial processes. In order to address this, this website includes multiple images of wind turbines in different states to provide a full perspective. Information is provided so that the user is able to gain a full picture of wind turbines and enough technical information to be able to make an informed opinion, rather than just based on aesthetics.

      Garrett Hardin's "The Tragedy of the Commons"

      Garret Hardin's "The Tragedy of the Commons" adds a more theoretical foundation for understanding the ideas described by Howe and Lennon. Hardin discusses the situation in which individuals pursue benefits from shared resources while the costs are distributed across the community. For example, he explains, "Each man is locked into a system that compels him to increase his herd without limit in a world that is limited." His analogy with grazing land applies directly to energy, but in the opposite way. Society benefits directly from electricity and reduced emissions. However, communities closest to this new kind of renewable energy infrastructure often experience negative side effects such as noise, habitat disruption, and altered landscapes. In this case, the benefits are spread out, while the burdens are more concentrated. Harden's writing really shows that there can be no energy system that is free of burdens or conflicts. No matter where the energy comes from, somewhere someone bears the cost, even if it is reduced compared to other ways of generating energy. Hardin shows that resource decisions always involve tradeoffs because all resources are ultimately limited. Decisions about where to place wind turbines or how many to install involve negotiation between landowners and corporations, but it is also important to look at the effects at the community level. When using the tool, it is important to take into consideration where the turbines are located, which certain communities they could be affecting, and how the benefits and harm of the project may be distributed across the regions.

      Conclusion

      The motivation behind this calculator is to create a clear way for users to understand renewable energy through self-exploration. Much discussion about wind power often relies on simplified images or generalized claims that mask the complexities of these forms of energy production under the name of sustainability, as outlined by the aforementioned authors. It often focuses on the projected environmental benefits without displaying the costs that are associated with these benefits. The goal is to respond to this gap in general understanding by offering a tool that makes it possible to examine wind energy from multiple perspectives. It doesn't present information in a persuasive manner, rather, it encourages reflection. The user is invited to take all the information in order to form their own opinions and position on wind energy. However, more important than just coming to a conclusion, the calculator illustrates a broader picture of wind energy.
